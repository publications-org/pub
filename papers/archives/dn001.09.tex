\section{Abstract}
The note covers several formulas that have been written down on February 17, 2026, for reusability purposes.

\section{Definitions}
Assuming, these definitions are valid:
\subsection{Definition of $C \in \mathbb{R}$:}
We define that for $C(n, p)$:
\begin{equation}
C(n, p) \coloneq \sum_{k=1}^{n} \sum_{d=1}^{p} I_{1} (k \cdot d) \in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}
Where $p > S(x) > n$. If and only if $x \geq 0$. Where $S(x)$ exists in $C(n ,p)$.

\subsection{Definition of $I_{1} \in \mathbb{C}$:}
We define that for $I_{1}(t)$:
\begin{equation}
I_{1}(t) \coloneq \oint \frac{|Log(t\cdot z)|}{t}dz, \quad \forall z \in \mathbb{R}, \quad t > 0
\end{equation}
Where $Log$ is the Principal Complex Logarithm of: $t \cdot z$, and $z \in \mathbb{C}$.

\section{Theorems}

Stating the following theorems per [1.1], and [1.2] definitions:

\subsection{Theorem One:} 

$C(n, p) \in \mathbb{R}$ is a real number for all $n, p \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $p > S(x) > n$ and $x \geq 0$.

\subsection{Theorem Two:} 

$I_{1}(t) \in \mathbb{C}$ is a complex number for all $t > 0$ and $t \in \mathbb{C}$.

\section{Conclusion}

The goal of this note was to establish the definitions of $C(n, p)$ and $I_{1}(t)$, and to state the theorems that follow from these definitions. The properties of these functions can be further explored in future work.
